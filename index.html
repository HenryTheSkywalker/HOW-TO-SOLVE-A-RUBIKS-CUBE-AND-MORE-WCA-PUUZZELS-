    Tips & Tricks

    Beginner essentials
    1 Always start with the same color
    Pick white as your starting face and stick with it. Muscle memory builds faster when the starting position is always the same. Almost all tutorials use white first.
    2 Learn the "sexy move" first
    R U R' U' — this is used in nearly every step. Practice it until your fingers can do it without thinking. It should eventually take less than 1 second.
    3 Don't peel the stickers
    Sounds obvious, but many beginners try to "cheat." Peeling stickers ruins the cube. If you're stuck, there's always a solution — rewatch the step you're on.
    4 Use a good cube
    A budget speedcube (like a MoYu RS3M or QiYi Warrior) makes a massive difference versus a cheap toy store cube. Smooth turning = faster learning.
    5 Color scheme matters
    Standard cubes have white opposite yellow, red opposite orange, blue opposite green. Knowing this helps you find pieces without rotating the whole cube.
    Getting faster
    6 Use finger tricks, not wrist turns
    Push the R face with your right index finger, pull U with your right wrist/index. Your whole hand should barely move. Finger tricks cut your time in half.
    7 Look ahead while solving
    While your fingers execute one step, your eyes should already be searching for the next piece. This is the #1 speed improvement for beginners.
    8 Time yourself from day one
    Use a timer (csTimer or Twisty Timer app). Tracking your times shows progress and keeps you motivated. Don't worry about speed — just track it.
    Common mistakes to avoid
    ! Rotating the whole cube too much
    Every full cube rotation wastes time and breaks your spatial awareness. Try to solve from one orientation as much as possible. Use U moves to bring pieces to you.
    ! Solving one face, not one layer
    The white face must have matching side colors too. A white face where the sides don't match is NOT a solved first layer. Always check the sides.
    ! Moving the cube during Step 7
    During the final corner orientation step, only turn U to bring corners to the working position. Rotating the whole cube will scramble everything.

    CFOP Tips & Tricks

    Cross optimization
    1 Plan the ENTIRE cross in inspection
    Top cubers plan all 4 cross edges (and sometimes the first F2L pair) in the 15-second inspection. Practice by scrambling, planning with eyes open, then executing with eyes closed.
    2 Use cross-color neutrality
    Instead of always starting on white, check all 6 colors during inspection and pick the easiest cross. This can save 2–4 moves on average. Start with dual-neutral (white/yellow).
    3 Insert cross edges relative to each other
    Don't solve edges one at a time. Keyhole technique: insert one edge, then use the empty slot to solve the next without breaking the first. Think in pairs.
    F2L mastery
    4 Solve F2L pairs in the back first
    Solving back slots first means the front is clear for lookahead. You can see the next pair forming while your fingers work on the current one.
    5 Learn to solve pairs from every angle
    Don't always rotate the cube to put the slot in front-right. Learn to insert into all 4 slots from different angles. Cube rotations are time-wasters.
    6 Track two pieces at once
    Keep your eyes on both the corner and edge of your current pair. When one goes out of sight (behind the cube), track the other. Never lose both.
    7 Empty slot tricks
    If a pair's corner is already in the slot but twisted, don't just pull it out and re-insert. There are faster 2-gen solutions. If the edge is in the wrong slot, pair it with a different corner first, then swap.
    OLL & PLL speedups
    8 Learn 2-look before full OLL/PLL
    Master the ~10 algorithms for 2-look OLL + 2-look PLL perfectly before expanding. Speed from fewer algorithms executed well beats knowing all 78 algorithms slowly.
    9 PLL recognition by headlights + bar
    Look at the left side first for "headlights" (two matching corners), then check the front "bar" (matching edge). This two-step system identifies all 21 PLLs instantly.
    10 AUF prediction
    During PLL execution, predict the final U adjustment (AUF) before you finish. This eliminates the pause between PLL end and timer stop. Practice by calling out AUF mid-algorithm.

    Roux Tips & Tricks

    1 Master M-slice speed
    Roux lives and dies by M-slice speed. Use your left ring finger for M and left middle finger for M'. Drill M U M' U sequences until they're automatic. This is your competitive edge.
    2 Plan the entire first block
    Like CFOP's cross, your FB should be fully planned in inspection. Start with the DL edge, then visualize the two corner-edge pairs. Target: 9 moves or fewer.
    3 Use "wide R" moves for second block
    r moves (Rw) are equivalent to R + M', which can set up SB pairs while also moving M-slice pieces. This lets you influence LSE during SB.
    4 Learn EO recognition patterns
    During LSE, an edge is "bad" if it has an F/B color on the U/D face or a U/D color on the F/B face. Practice spotting bad edges instantly — this is pure pattern recognition.
    5 Roux is naturally OH-friendly
    If you plan to do one-handed solving, Roux is ideal. The restricted moveset (R, r, U, M) is perfect for right-hand-only execution. Many top OH solvers use Roux.
    6 Don't neglect SB efficiency
    Beginners often have a fast FB but slow SB. The key: solve the DR edge first (1–3 moves), then pair corners and edges using R/U/M moves. Keep SB under 15 moves.

    2x2 Tips & Tricks

    1 The 2x2 is all about the first layer
    With Ortega/CLL, the last layer is one algorithm. Your first layer speed is 90% of your solve time. Practice planning the entire first face in inspection.
    2 Learn to be color neutral
    On a 2x2, being color neutral is much easier than on 3x3 and saves significantly more time. Check all 6 possible first faces and pick the one that takes fewest moves (often 1–3).
    3 Upgrade from Ortega to CLL quickly
    Ortega is great for learning, but CLL is only 42 algorithms and eliminates an entire step. You can learn 3–4 CLL cases per day and know them all in two weeks.
    4 Predict CLL during first layer
    Advanced 2x2 solvers recognize the CLL case during first layer insertion. As you place the last corner, note where the top-layer corners are. This eliminates the recognition pause.
    5 The 2x2 is great for warming up
    Before a 3x3 session, do 10–15 quick 2x2 solves. It warms up your fingers and recognition without being mentally exhausting. Great for competition warm-ups too.
    ! Don't over-turn
    The 2x2 is small and light — it's easy to overshoot turns. Practice controlled, precise turning. A lockup on a 2-second solve costs you 25% of your time.

    2. Use Rw (wide R) and Uw (wide U) to move pieces without breaking solved centers.
    3. Solve opposite faces first, then adjacent pairs.

    Entirely intuitive. The key trick: when inserting the last center pieces, use a "hide and restore" approach — move the target piece to the correct face, then undo any damage.

    Step 2

    Pair Edges

    Match the two edge pieces of each color combo to form single "fat" edges (like 3x3 edges). There are 12 edge pairs to solve.

    Basic edge pairing technique

    1. Place one edge piece on the front-right using U/Uw moves.
    2. Find its partner. Bring it to the front-left position.
    3. Use Uw' to join them. Then L' to insert a new edge. Then Uw to restore.
    Basic edge pairingUw' L' Uw (or mirrored: Uw R Uw')

    For the last 3 edges, you'll need a different technique since there aren't spare unsolved edges to swap. Use "slice-flip-slice" to pair the final edges.

    Step 3

    Solve Like a 3x3

    With centers and edges done, the 4x4 behaves like a 3x3. Use your preferred 3x3 method (beginner LBL or CFOP).

    Clock — Beginner Method

    Clock is a unique WCA puzzle with 14 small clock faces, 4 pins, and 4 wheels. The goal is to set all 14 clocks to 12 o'clock. No algorithms in the traditional sense — it's all about understanding the mechanism.

    4 Steps
    0Algorithms
    15–45sSolve time
    Easy
    ð Buy a Clock
    Step 1

    Understand the Mechanism

    The Clock has two sides, each showing 9 clock faces arranged in a 3×3 grid. Four pins connect the two sides. Four wheels (one at each corner) turn the clocks.

    • Pins UP: the wheel turns the adjacent clocks on the current side.
    • Pins DOWN: the wheel turns the adjacent clocks on the opposite side.
    • Each wheel affects a group of clocks depending on which pins are up or down.
    • The 4 corner clocks on each side are shared with the opposite face (they are the same physical dial).

    Spend a few minutes experimenting with different pin configurations and turning wheels to build intuition for what each combination does.

    Step 2

    Solve the Front Corners

    Set the pins to isolate individual clocks, then turn the appropriate wheel to set each corner clock to 12.

    • Put one pin down and the other three up. This isolates one corner clock.
    • Turn the wheel nearest to that pin to set that corner clock to 12.
    • Repeat for each of the 4 corners on the front side.

    After this step, the 4 corner clocks on the front should all point to 12.

    Step 3

    Solve the Front Edges and Center

    With the front corners solved, adjust the 4 edge clocks and 1 center clock.

    • Set two adjacent pins down to control an edge clock. Turn the corresponding wheel to set it to 12.
    • Set all four pins down to control only the center clock. Turn any wheel to set it to 12.

    The front face should now be fully solved with all 9 clocks at 12.

    Step 4

    Flip and Solve the Back

    Flip the puzzle over. The 4 corner clocks are already solved (they're shared with the front). Now solve the remaining 5 clocks (4 edges + 1 center) using the same pin technique.

    • One pin down + turn wheel → adjusts a corner region (but corners are done, so use this to fix edges).
    • Two adjacent pins down → isolates an edge clock.
    • All pins down → controls only the center.

    Clock is often the most divisive WCA event — people either love it or find it unusual. Top solvers average around 4–5 seconds, making it one of the fastest events to solve.

    Letter Scheme & Memo Basics

    Assign a letter (A–X) to each sticker position on the cube (except centers). A common scheme labels stickers going around each face.

    • There are 24 edge stickers (12 edges × 2) and 24 corner stickers (8 corners × 3).
    • Buffer pieces are the starting points: typically UR edge (sticker B) and ULB corner (sticker A).
    • You'll trace "cycles" by following where each piece needs to go, building a chain of letters.
    • Convert letter pairs into memorable images or words for faster memorization.

    Before learning execution, practice the memo step sighted (eyes open). Being fluent at tracing is essential before going blindfolded.

    Step 2

    Memo Edges

    Starting from the buffer edge (UR), trace the edge cycle:

    1. Look at the piece currently in the buffer position.
    2. Determine where that piece belongs — the letter of its target sticker becomes the first letter of your memo.
    3. Go to that target location and read the piece there. Its target becomes the next letter.
    4. Continue until you return to the buffer. If you hit a solved piece or the buffer before all edges are done, start a new cycle from any unsolved edge.

    Your edge memo will be a sequence of letters, e.g., D F L Q H S, which you group into letter pairs: DF LQ HS.

    Step 3

    Memo Corners

    Same process as edges but starting from the buffer corner (ULB). Trace the corner cycle and record the letters.

    • Corners have 3 stickers each, so pay attention to which specific sticker you're reading.
    • Again, group letters into pairs and convert to images.
    • Memo corners after edges (but execute corners first), so corner memo needs to be recalled first.

    Many BLD solvers use a "room" or "journey" memory palace for edges and a simple story or audio loop for corners.

    Step 4

    Execute Corners (Y-perm)

    Put on the blindfold. Execute corners first using the Y-perm to swap the buffer corner with each target.

    Y-permR U' R' U' R U R' F' R U R' U' R' F R

    For each letter in your corner memo:

    1. Perform setup moves to bring the target sticker into the swap position (RFD corner, sticker facing right).
    2. Execute the Y-perm.
    3. Undo the setup moves (in reverse).

    Some targets (like the sticker already at RFD-right) need no setup at all. Build a table of setup moves for each letter.

    Step 5

    Execute Edges (T-perm)

    After all corners are done, execute edges using the T-perm to swap the buffer edge with each target.

    T-permR U R' U' R' F R2 U' R' U' R U R' F'

    For each letter in your edge memo:

    1. Perform setup moves to bring the target sticker into the swap position (UR edge, sticker facing up).
    2. Execute the T-perm.
    3. Undo the setup moves.

    Old Pochmann is the gateway to blindfolded solving. Once comfortable, upgrade to M2/OP (faster edges using M-slice moves) or 3-Style (the method top BLD solvers use, with direct 3-cycles).

    3x3 One-Handed — OH CFOP

    One-handed solving uses the same CFOP method as two-handed, but with adapted fingertricks and move preferences. The key is mastering R/U-based algorithms and efficient grip.

    4 Steps
    Same as CFOP
    15–30sSolve time
    Intermediate
    Step 1

    Grip & Turning Technique

    The foundation of OH solving is a proper grip and efficient turning.

    • Standard OH grip: pinky and ring finger on the bottom, thumb on the front, index finger on top. The middle finger supports the back.
    • R moves: push with index finger (R) or pull with ring finger (R').
    • U moves: push with index finger (U) or flick with thumb (U').
    • D moves: use the table — push the bottom layer against the surface.
    • F moves: avoid when possible; use thumb to push if needed.
    • Use z rotations to convert L moves into R moves, which are much easier OH.

    Practice just turning R U R' U' one-handed for 5 minutes daily until it becomes fluid. This is the single most important OH drill.

    Step 2

    OH Cross

    Solve the cross one-handed. The technique is the same as two-handed CFOP, but adjust your approach:

    • Plan the entire cross during inspection — this is even more important in OH since turning is slower.
    • Favor R and U moves in your cross solutions when possible.
    • D-layer moves can be done with table assistance: press the bottom layer against the table and rotate.
    • Aim for 8 moves or fewer for the cross solution.
    Step 3

    OH F2L

    F2L (First Two Layers) is where the biggest time differences between OH and 2H occur. Key adaptations:

    • Favor R U triggers over F moves. Instead of U R U' R' U' F' U F, try to use alternative insertions that avoid F.
    • Empty slot techniques: insert pairs into the back slots first so you can use R U moves.
    • Rotate to avoid L/F: a y rotation followed by R moves is often faster than an L move.
    Basic insert (right)R U R'
    Basic insert (front-right)R U' R' U R U R'
    Avoid-F alternativeR U2 R' U R U R' (instead of f R f')

    OH F2L is all about minimizing rotations and F/B moves. Every F move costs time — look for R/U-only solutions.

    Step 4

    OH OLL & PLL

    Use the same OLL/PLL algorithms as two-handed, but prefer OH-friendly versions:

    Sune (OH-friendly)R U R' U R U2 R'
    AntisuneR U2 R' U' R U' R'
    T-permR U R' U' R' F R2 U' R' U' R U R' F'
    U-perm (a)R U R' U R' U' R2 U' R' U R' U R
    • R U R' and R U2 R' are extremely fast one-handed — algorithms built from these triggers are ideal.
    • Some PLLs have OH-specific algorithms. For example, many solvers use different G-perm algorithms OH.
    • Algorithms with F moves (like some F-perms) may need alternatives or at least practice with thumb pushes.

    World-class OH solvers average under 10 seconds. The current top solvers use CFOP or ZZ (which eliminates F moves entirely after EOCross).

    Fewest Moves Challenge — Blockbuilding Basics

    FMC is about finding the shortest solution to a scramble. Unlike speedsolving, every move counts. The core technique is blockbuilding combined with EO analysis, NISS, and insertions.

    5 Steps
    Conceptual
    60 minTime limit
    Advanced
    Step 1

    Edge Orientation (EO) Analysis

    Before building blocks, analyze the edge orientation of the scramble. EO tells you which edges are "good" and which are "bad" relative to a chosen axis.

    • On the F/B axis, an edge is bad if it requires an F or B move to be solved. Count the bad edges.
    • Fixing all bad edges early (in 2–4 moves) drastically reduces the remaining move count.
    • Look for moves that fix many bad edges at once — a single F move can fix up to 4 bad edges.

    Good EO analysis is the #1 skill that separates intermediate FMC solvers from advanced ones. Practice counting bad edges on scrambles until it becomes second nature.

    Step 2

    Build a 2×2×2 Block

    Find and build a 2×2×2 block (a solved corner with its 3 adjacent edges) in as few moves as possible.

    • Look for pairs already formed in the scramble — a pre-made pair can be extended to a 2×2×2 in 1–2 moves.
    • Try multiple starting blocks in different positions. FMC is about exploration — try at least 3–5 different starts.
    • Aim for 3–4 moves for this step.
    Step 3

    Extend to 2×2×3 Block

    Expand the 2×2×2 into a 2×2×3 block by adding another layer of pieces.

    • This means solving 2 more edges and 1 more corner adjacent to the existing block.
    • Look for pieces that are already near their target positions.
    • Aim for 3–5 moves for this extension, making the running total about 7–9 moves for the 2×2×3.
    Example efficiencyScramble-dependent (no fixed algorithms in FMC)
    Step 4

    Finish F2L & Last Layer

    Complete the remaining first-two-layer pieces (the "F2L minus block" portion), then solve the last layer.

    • Continue blockbuilding to complete F2L in as few moves as possible.
    • For the last layer, look for short algorithm sequences. Common efficient LL solutions:
      • OLL + PLL can sometimes be short if you're lucky.
      • ZBLL (solve LL in one look after EO) occasionally gives very short solutions.
      • Leave 3–5 unsolved pieces and use insertions (Step 5) instead.

    Aim for a total skeleton of about 25–35 moves before insertions.

    Step 5

    NISS & Insertions

    Two powerful techniques that define competitive FMC solving:

    NISS (Normal-Inverse Scramble Switching)

    • At any point in your solve, you can switch to working on the inverse scramble.
    • Write your current solution, reverse it, append it to the inverse scramble, and continue solving from there.
    • This often reveals easier blockbuilding opportunities that aren't visible on the normal scramble.

    Insertions

    • If your solution skeleton leaves 3 unsolved corners (a 3-cycle), you can find a commutator to solve them.
    • Go through your skeleton move by move, and at each point check if a short commutator (e.g., [R U' R', D] = R U' R' D R U R' D') will solve the remaining pieces.
    • Good insertions save 4–6 moves because moves from the commutator cancel with adjacent moves in the skeleton.

    Competitive FMC results average 25–30 moves. The key is exploring many different starting blocks (try at least 10), using NISS to find shortcuts, and finding insertions with maximum cancellation.

    • Tips are independent — turning one never affects any other piece.
    • Do this during inspection or at the start of the solve.
    Step 2

    First Layer with Keyhole

    • The top layer can usually be solved intuitively or with a simple trigger.

    Top twist (left)L' U' L U' L' U' L

    Clock — Simultaneous Method

    The Simultaneous method minimizes flips by solving multiple clock faces at once, using pin combinations to isolate and control specific dials. Ideally requires only 1 flip.

    4 Steps
    No algs(intuitive)
    6–10sSolve time
    Intermediate
    Step 1

    Pin Setup & Notation

    Understand the pin system. The Clock has 4 pins labeled UL, UR, DL, DR. Each pin can be "up" (pushed in) or "down" (pulled out). Pins are shared between front and back.

    • Pushed in (up): the wheel on that side controls its own clock plus adjacent clocks.
    • Pulled out (down): the wheel is disconnected from the back face.
    • Different pin combinations let you isolate specific dials for independent control.

    Spend time learning which pin combinations affect which dials. This understanding is the foundation of all fast Clock methods.

    Step 2

    Solve 4 Corners at Once

    Push all pins down (pulled out). In this configuration, each wheel only affects its own corner clock and the adjacent edge clocks.

    • Turn each of the 4 wheels independently to set the corner clocks to 12.
    • Each wheel primarily controls its corner — solve all 4 corners on the front face.
    • The edge clocks will be partially affected, but don't worry about them yet.
    Step 3

    Solve Edges + Center

    Use specific pin combinations to isolate edge and center clocks without disturbing the solved corners.

    • UL+UR up (DL, DR down): turning the top wheels controls the top edge clock.
    • UL+DL up (UR, DR down): turning the left wheels controls the left edge clock.
    • Continue with each edge until all 4 edges read 12.
    • Finally, push all pins up and adjust any wheel to solve the center clock (all wheels move the center equally).

    The key insight: with the right pin combination, you can adjust one edge without disturbing corners or other edges. Master these pin patterns and the solve becomes intuitive.

    Step 4

    Flip and Fix the Back

    Flip the Clock over and repeat the same process for the back. In the Simultaneous method, you aim to minimize flips to just 1.

    • Because pins are shared, some of your front-face solving already partially solved the back.
    • After flipping, assess which clocks are still off and apply the same corner → edge → center technique.
    • Advanced solvers can plan the back-face adjustments while solving the front, making the flip almost seamless.

    World-class Clock solvers average under 4 seconds. The key is minimizing the flip — solve as much of both faces as possible before flipping, then quickly fix the remaining dials.

    3x3 BLD — M2/R2 Method

    M2/R2 improves on Old Pochmann by using M2 moves for edge execution, cutting move count significantly. Corners still use Old Pochmann's Y-perm approach.

    4 Steps
    2–3Algorithms
    1–3 minSolve time
    Intermediate
    Step 1

    Letter Scheme

    Assign a letter A–X to each sticker on the cube (excluding buffer stickers). This is the same system used in Old Pochmann.

    • Edge buffer: UF (the sticker at the intersection of U and F faces) for the M2 method. Some solvers use DF instead.
    • Corner buffer: ULB (same as Old Pochmann).
    • Common scheme: Speffz — A=ULB, B=UB, C=URB, D=UR, etc., going clockwise around each face.

    Your letter scheme is permanent once learned, so choose one and stick with it. Speffz is the most popular and has the most community resources.

    Step 2

    Memorization

    Trace edge targets and corner targets into letter pairs, just like Old Pochmann. The tracing process is identical.

    • Start at the buffer (UF for edges, ULB for corners) and follow the cycle.
    • Note the letter of each sticker you visit, forming pairs: AB, CD, EF, etc.
    • Use visual images (letter pairs become words/pictures) and place them in a memory palace for longer memo.
    • Memo edges first, then corners (you'll execute corners first, then edges).

    M2 memo is the same as OP memo. The only difference is execution, so if you already know Old Pochmann, you only need to learn new execution for edges.

    Step 3

    Execute Edges with M2

    Instead of using T-perms, swap the buffer edge (UF) with each target using setup + M2 + undo setup. This is much faster than OP edge execution.

    • Non-M-slice targets: use setup moves to bring the target to the UB or DF position, perform M2, then undo setup.
    • M-slice targets (UB, UF, DB, DF): require special handling since M2 directly interacts with them.
    M2M2 (turn M slice 180°)

    Example: target at BL[U' L U] M2 [U' L' U] (setup, M2, undo)
    • UB target: just do M2 directly (no setup needed).
    • DB target: setup to UB first with [M' U2 M U2] or just do M2 with adjusted setup.
    • Targets on even vs. odd positions in the memo affect whether you do M2 or M2 + adjustment.

    The M2 method roughly halves the edge execution move count compared to Old Pochmann. Most intermediate BLD solvers use M2 for edges as their first upgrade from OP.

    Step 4

    Execute Corners with Old Pochmann

    Corner execution remains the same as Old Pochmann: use setup moves + Y-perm to swap the buffer corner (ULB) with each target.

    Y-permR U' R' U' R U R' F' R U R' U' R' F R
    • Setup the target corner to the RDF position (the swap target of Y-perm from ULB buffer).
    • Apply Y-perm, then undo the setup moves.
    • Repeat for each pair of letters in your corner memo.

    Once comfortable with M2 corners, the next upgrade is learning 3-style (commutators) for corners, eliminating Y-perm entirely. This is the method used by sub-20-second BLD solvers.

    A corner-turning puzzle with 4 movable corners and 6 center pieces. Simple but fun. Can be solved intuitively in just a few moves.

    Understand the Moves

    The Ivy Cube has 4 corners that rotate. Each move turns a corner and the 3 center pieces surrounding it. Name the corners A, B, C, D (or use the face they're closest to).

    • Each corner has only 2 positions: solved or turned once.

    After corners are solved, solve the 12 edge pieces. Many edges can be placed intuitively by turning corners to cycle edges around.

    1. Find an edge piece on the top that belongs on the bottom (or vice versa).

    The Redi Cube is proof that "only one algorithm" puzzles can still be incredibly satisfying. The trick is learning which setup moves let you apply the 3-cycle to any edges you need.

    Mastermorphix (3x3 Shape Mod)

    A tetrahedral shape mod of the 3x3. It looks completely alien when scrambled but uses all the same algorithms. The challenge is recognizing which pieces go where when the shape is so different.

    Step 1

    Identify Piece Types

    The Mastermorphix has the same piece types as a 3x3, just shaped differently:

    • 4 tips = 3x3 corners (the pointy tips of the tetrahedron)
    • 4 large triangles = 3x3 centers
    • 6 edge pieces = 3x3 edges
    • 4 inner corners = also 3x3 corners (the "inverted" tips)

    The Mastermorphix is the ultimate 3x3 shape mod challenge. If you can solve this without a tutorial, you truly understand 3x3 mechanics at a deep level.

    1. Start by finding the widest layer — this is the layer with the thickest center.

    Universal Tips & Tricks

    These apply to every puzzle and every method. The foundation of fast cubing.

    Speed & technique
    1 Turn accurately, not fast
    A locked-up turn costs more time than a slightly slower, precise turn. Focus on turning quality first — speed will come naturally. Aim for zero lockups before aiming for speed.
    2 Lookahead is everything
    The time between steps (pausing to find the next piece) is where all the time is lost. Train yourself to find the next piece while your hands are still executing the current step. Slow down your turning to practice this.
    3 Use both hands equally
    Most beginners favor the right hand. Practice left-hand triggers (L' U' L U) with the same fluency as right-hand ones. This doubles your options and halves cube rotations.
    4 Minimize cube rotations
    Every time you rotate the whole cube, you lose 0.3–0.5 seconds and break your tracking. Elite solvers average under 2 rotations per solve. Learn to insert pairs from all angles.
    Practice methods
    5 Do targeted drills
    Don't just do full solves. Practice individual steps in isolation. Scramble, solve just the cross, rescramble. Do 50 crosses in a row. This focused practice beats 50 full solves for skill building.
    6 Slow solving > fast solving for learning
    When learning a new technique, solve at 50% speed with zero pauses. If you have to pause to find a piece, you're going too fast. Continuous slow turning builds lookahead better than sprinting.
    7 Record and review your solves
    Film yourself solving and watch it back at 0.5x speed. You'll spot unnecessary rotations, slow recognition, and bad finger tricks you never noticed in real-time.
    8 Use algorithm trainers
    Websites like csTimer and apps like Twisty Timer have OLL/PLL/CLL trainers that give you specific cases to practice. Use them to drill your weakest algorithms instead of hoping they come up in solves.
    9 The "Ao5 / Ao12" mindset
    Don't obsess over single-solve PBs. Track your average of 5 (Ao5) and average of 12 (Ao12) — these show real improvement. A consistent Ao12 drop means you've genuinely gotten faster.
    Advanced concepts
    10 Learn to recognize, not recite
    Advanced cubers don't think "okay, that's the T-perm." They see a pattern and their fingers react automatically. Build pattern → muscle memory links through deliberate repetition. 50+ reps per algorithm.
    11 Warm up before speed sessions
    Do 5–10 slow, deliberate solves before timing yourself. Cold fingers and cold brain = lockups and recognition failures. Warm up physically and mentally.
    12 Competition nerves are real
    Practice solving in front of people or under pressure. Time yourself with stakes (e.g., if I don't sub-X, I have to do 20 more). Competition performance improves with exposure to pressure.
    Mistakes everyone makes
    ! Learning algorithms before understanding them
    Memorizing an algorithm without understanding what it does makes you unable to adapt when things go wrong. Always understand the purpose before memorizing the moves.
    ! Skipping steps in your progression
    Don't jump to full OLL (57 algs) before mastering 2-look OLL. Don't learn ZB before sub-15 CFOP. Each level builds on the last. Skipping creates gaps that limit your ceiling.
    ! Neglecting hardware setup
    A cube that's too tight, too loose, or too slow handicaps you. Learn to tension and lubricate your cube. Silicone-based lubes (like Traxxas 50k) for the core, water-based (like DNM-37) for the pieces.
    ! Only doing Ao100s without analysis
    Grinding 100 solves without thought is practice without learning. After every bad solve, ask: "What went wrong? Was it a bad cross? Slow recognition?" Fix the weakest link, not just the total time.
